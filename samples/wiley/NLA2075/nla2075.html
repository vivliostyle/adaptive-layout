Summary

In data science, data are often represented by using an undirected graph where vertices represent objects and edges describe a relationship between two objects. In many applications, there can be many relations arising from different sources and/or different types of models. Clustering of multiple undirected graphs over the same set of vertices can be studied. Existing clustering methods of multiple graphs involve costly optimization and/or tensor computation. In this paper, we study block spectral clustering methods for these multiple graphs. The main contribution of this paper is to propose and construct block Laplacian matrices for clustering of multiple graphs. We present a novel variant of the Laplacian matrix called the block intra‐normalized Laplacian and prove the conditions required for zero eigenvalues in this variant. We also show that eigenvectors of the constructed block Laplacian matrix can be shown to be solutions of the relaxation of multiple graphs cut problems, and the lower and upper bounds of the optimal solutions of multiple graphs cut problems can also be established. Experimental results are given to demonstrate that the clustering accuracy and the computational time of the proposed method are better than those of tested clustering methods for multiple graphs.

1INTRODUCTION

In data mining, data sets are often represented as an undirected graph with vertices representing objects and (weighted or unweighted) edges describing a relationship between the objects. In many applications, these relationships are of different types and/or are obtained from different sources. Multiple graphs over the same vertex set are generated, and their edges from different graphs describe different relationships between objects. For instance, in bioinformatics, gene expression data under different organisms or different conditions are studied for biological knowledge discovery process.1 Each graph refers to each organism or condition over the same set of genes. An edge may be the correlation between two genes in the expression data. Publication data under different journals or conferences are studied for clustering of researchers into different research groups. Each graph refers to each journal or conference over the same set of researchers. An edge may be the relationship (e.g., co‐authorship or citation) between two researchers; see for instance Cai et al.2 and Kolda and Bader.3 In web analysis, multiple graphs over the same set of webpages can be generated by different semantic meanings. An edge may be the link between two webpages through a particular semantic meaning; see for instance Kolda and Bader,3 Kolda et al.,4 Li et al.,5 and Ng et al.6 Multiple graphs can be generated from different social networks over the same set of users. An edge may refer to an activity (e.g., photo sharing or topics discussion) between two users in a social network.7-10 This kind of multiple relation graphs can provide a better way of understanding, classifying, and grouping objects for analysis and applications. In the literature, multiview clustering has been developed for this kind of clustering problem, see for instance in Section 2 and the other literature.11–19

1.1The contribution

In this paper, we propose and develop a new approach for clustering of multiple graphs, where we seek K clusters on each graph by integrating the information from multiple graphs. Let us use an example to illustrate our idea. In Figure 1, we show the same set of eight people in two social sites: “Facebook” and “Google+.” Within the “Facebook” site, we construct a graph G1 corresponding the interactions among eight people. Similarly, we construct a graph G2 corresponding the interactions among the same eight people in the “Google+” site. For G1, we have three groups: C1(1)={1,2}, C1(2)={3,4,5}, C1(3)={6,7,8}. For G2, we have three groups: C2(1)={1,2,3}, C2(2)={4,5}, C2(3)={6,7,8}. We refer these clusters to Figure 2. Except the person numbered “3,” the other people belong to the same clusters G1 and G2. This example motivates us to design an algorithm for finding clusters (not necessary the same) in each graph. Thus, we assume that clusters in different graphs are similar but not necessarily the same. Such assumption is different from the other existing methods11–19 that seek the same clustering for all graphs.

In our approach, we study clustering for multiple graphs and each person can belong to different clusters in different graphs. Firstly, we represent the two graphs G1 and G2 as adjacency matrices as the (1,1)‐block and (2,2)‐block of a block matrix in Figure 1. Because we deal with the same set of eight people, we assign β in the main diagonal entries of (1,2)‐block and (2,1)‐block of the block matrix. The value of β can be used to control the consistency of the clustering results in the two graphs. This assignment refers to the relationships between “Facebook” and “Google+” sites. We view the whole 2‐by‐2 block matrix as the whole adjacency matrix of the eight people from two social sites and employ the block matrix for clustering. In Section 3, we will give a detailed discussion of the proposed method.

For the construction of block Laplacian matrices from multiple graphs, we show that the multiplicity of the zero eigenvalue of the constructed block Laplacian matrix is equal to the number of connected components in multiple graphs. We demonstrate that eigenvectors of the constructed block Laplacian matrix can be effectively used for clustering of vertices in each graph. It is important that we can make use of eigenvectors to obtain consistent clustering among different graphs. Also eigenvectors of such block Laplacian matrix can be shown to be solutions of the relaxation of multiple graphs cut problems. The lower and upper bounds of the optimal solutions of multiple graphs cut problem are studied and established. Finally, experimental results on synthetic and real data sets are given to demonstrate that the clustering accuracy and the computational time of the proposed method are better than those of tested clustering methods for multiple graphs in the literature.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we give a review of clustering methods of multiple graphs. In Section 3, we present the proposed framework and the algorithm. Theoretical results are shown for block Laplacian matrices. In Section ,4 we study multiple graphs cut problems and their relaxations. The lower and upper bounds of the optimal solutions of multiple graphs cut problems are given. In Section 5, we show numerical examples on synthetic and real data sets and demonstrate the usefulness of the proposed method. Finally, some concluding remarks are given in Section 6.

2RELATED WORK

In this section, we review related works of clustering methods for multiple graphs.

There are two approaches. One is based on prior statistical assumptions in multiple graphs. Probabilistic models aim to find the proper distribution for each cluster in all graphs, while maximizing the consistency of the same clusters distribution across different graphs. Expectation–maximization algorithms are usually employed to solve these models. Airoldi et al.18 proposed a mixed membership stochastic block model for relational data with the assumption that the ensemble of mixed membership vectors can govern the relationships among objects. The model allows each object to have fractional memberships in several communities. Anandkumar et al.16 proposed a unified approach to learning mixed membership community models by using method‐of‐moments estimators and tensor spectral decomposition. Shiga and Mamitsuka17 extended a single graph probabilistic model to multiple graphs probabilistic model by using a multinomial distribution. Their aim is to determine clusters in some graphs where they are not necessary to be consistent in all the given graphs. They employed variational Bayesian learning methods to finding such clusters in multiple graphs.

The second approach is based on the weighted combination of data, and multi‐view clustering algorithms are developed. Such methods aim to obtain a latent subspace shared by multiple views by assuming that different input views are generated from the same latent subspace. Bickel and Scheffer19 developed multiview spherical k‐means algorithm and multiview version of mixture‐of‐multinomials expectation minimization algorithms. Xie and Sun14 extended kernel k‐means methods in a multiview version, where its kernel matrix is the weighted sum of kernel matrices from different relations and sources. Liu et al.15 developed multiview partitioning via tensor methods. Their framework is to integrate multiview data in the context of tensors. The resulting optimization problem is to solve for shared eigenvectors of tensors based on high‐order singular value decomposition. The computed eigenvectors are used for clustering. Instead of using tensors, Tang et al.13 proposed Linked Matrix Factorization as a novel way of fusing information from multiple graphs for clustering. The optimization problem is required to solve for a common factor matrix for clustering purpose. In Kumar et al.,20 coregularization is employed to achieve the clustering hypotheses via a combined optimization problem. Dong et al.12 also designed a graph regularization method by minimizing the distance between eigenvectors (or clustering results) from multiple graphs. Kumar and Daumé III11 proposed to use co‐training for solving the problem of multiview spectral clustering. Eigenvectors (or clustering results) from one graph are used to update the structure of the other graphs. The training process is done iteratively until clustering results are stable.

All these methods assume that each object should belong to the same cluster in different graphs. In many applications, clusters in different graphs are similar but may be not be necessarily the same. For example, while detecting genes module for lung cancer, the cancer‐related gene modules in different subtypes of cancer share most of the common genes while they variate slightly for some special genes for different subtypes. In this way, we search the clustering of objects within each graph, while at the same time should be highly consistent across different graphs. In the next section, we propose to use simple but novel block spectral clustering methods for multiple graphs.

3BLOCK SPECTRAL CLUSTERING METHOD

3.1Spectral clustering

Given a set of N objects {xi}i=1N, a similarity si,j can be defined for each pair of objects xi and xj based on some similarity measure. A similarity matrix S can be formed, or a graph G = (V,E) is constructed in which the set V of vertices represents a set of objects and the set E of edges refers to connections between vertices. The adjacency matrix A can be constructed where A(i,j) is equal to 1 (or 0) when the i‐th vertex is (or not) connected to the j‐th vertex, or equal to w if it is connected with weight w. In some applications, A is just equal to S where the edge ei,j∈E between the i‐th vertex and the j‐th vertex is measured by si,j. The weighted degree of the i‐th vertex (for the i‐th object) is defined by di=∑j=1NA(i,j), that is, the sum of all the weights of edges connected to the i‐th vertex. The degree matrix D can be formed as a diagonal matrix where its main diagonal entries are d1,d2,…,dN.

The Laplacian matrix L is defined by D − A or the normalized Laplacian matrix L˜ is defined by I − D − 1/2AD − 1/2 where D − 1/2 refers to a matrix where its diagonal entries are taken one over the square root and I refers to the identity matrix. Let U˜ be the assignment of N objects into K clusters, where

U˜i,k=1ifi‐th object belongs to thekth cluster,0otherwise.

for i = 1,2,…,N, k = 1,2,…,K. Then we aim to find the minimizer N‐by‐K matrix U˜ for

minU˜∑k=1KU˜(:,k)tLU˜(:,k)U˜(:,k)tU˜(:,k)subject to∑k=1KU˜(:,k)=1.

By setting U(:,k)=U˜(:,k)/||U˜(:,k)||2, the above optimization problem is relaxed to

(1)minUtrace(UtLU)subject toUtU=I.

Here, U(:,k) refers to the k‐th column of U, trace(·) is the sum of the main diagonal entries of a matrix, and Ut denotes the transpose of U. 1 is a vector with all entries 1. Thus, spectral clustering is to find the minimizer N‐by‐K matrix U for trace(UtLU) with UtU = I. The solution of this optimization problem is given by the K eigenvectors corresponding to the K smallest eigenvalues of L. By clustering the N points into K clusters based on the K‐dimensional coordinate vector of each point, we can get the final clusters; see for instance.21 The optimization formulation 1 is relaxed to the Ratio Cut problem in von Luxburg,21 which explains spectral clustering from the graph cut point of view. For the normalized case using normalized L˜, the relaxed formulation is to find the minimizer N‐by‐K matrix U for

minUtrace(UtL˜U)subjecttoUtU=I

which is relaxed to the Normalized Cut problem21.

3.2Block Laplacian matrices

Suppose we have M multiple undirected graphs constructed from M different relations or sources: G1,G2,…,GM over the same set of N vertices. We also assume these M graphs have the same weight in the analysis. The adjacency matrix for Gm is Am, where Am(i,j) = 1 if there is an edge between the i‐th vertex and the j‐th vertex, otherwise Am(i,j) = 0. We note that Am is symmetric. We use Dm to denote the diagonal matrix with the diagonal entries being the weighted degrees of the corresponding vertices ( di(m) refers to the weighted degree of i‐th vertex in m‐th graph). Correspondingly, we have an N‐by‐N Laplacian matrix Lm from Am.

Let us assume that for the M graphs, each different graph Gm can be partitioned into K components {Cm(1),Cm(2),⋯,Cm(K)}, that is, ∪k=1KCm(k)=V and Cm(k)∩Cm(k′)=∅ for k ≠ k′. When C1(k1),C2(k2),⋯CM′(kM′) are connected together via self‐edges, and they are not connected to the other components via self‐edges, they are called an “across‐component.” Intuitively, these components are connected across multiple graphs via self‐edges. For the example in Figure 1, There are C1(1), C1(2), and C1(3) in G1 and C2(1), C2(2), and C2(3) in G2. After checking self‐edges, one can find that there are two across‐components: ( C1(1),C1(2),C2(1),C2(2)), and ( C1(3),C2(3)) as shown in Figure 2.

Once we consider B as a Laplacian matrix for a graph containing MN vertices, it is clear that an across‐component is a classical connected component in a graph with MN vertices. By using the results in spectral clustering,21 we obtain the following theorem that states that the multiplicity of the zero eigenvalue of B is equal to the number of across‐components in multiple graphs.

Theorem 1

(i) B is a symmetric positive semi‐definite matrix and zero is the smallest eigenvalue of B. (ii) The multiplicity of the zero eigenvalue of B is equal to the number of across‐components.

3.3Block intra‐normalized Laplacian matrices

In this subsection, we introduce a special form of normalized matrices as follows:

(5)B^=D−1/2BwD−1/2+βBa

where

D=D10⋯00D2⋯0⋮⋮⋱⋮00⋯DM.

Here, we normalize each inner Laplacian matrix in Bw. It is called a block intra‐normalized Laplacian matrix. The advantage of using this approach is that the normalized weighted degree of vertex in each graph is not mixed with M and β. β can be used to control the weight between the within and across matrices. Also, the constructed matrix in Equation 5 is exactly related to the realization for multiple graphs cuts problem (see the derivation in Section 4.1.2).

According to Theorem 1, we know that both Bw and Ba are positive semi‐definite matrices. It is easy to show that D−1/2BwD−1/2 is also a positive semi‐definite matrix. It implies that B^=D−1/2BwD−1/2+βBa is a positive semi‐definite matrix. By using the example with β = 1 in Figure 1, the eigenvalues of the block intra‐normalized Laplacian matrix are given by 0, 0.003, 0.121, 0.927, 1.500, 1.500, 1.719, 1.795, 2.000, 2.054, 2.398, 3.249, 3.500, 3.500, 3.781, and 3.954. The multiplicity of zero eigenvalue is equal to one that is not equal to the number of across‐components.

We note that the weighted degrees of the vertices are used in the construction of block intra‐normalized Laplacian matrices. In the following, we investigate how the number of zero eigenvalues is related to the cluster structure arising from block intra‐normalized matrices by considering the weighted degrees of the vertices. 4THE PROPOSED ALGORITHM

In this paper, we propose and formulate the following optimization problem for clustering of multiple graphs:

(9)minYtrace(YtBY)subject toYtY=I

where we assume that there are K clusters in the multiple graphs, Y=[Y1tY2t⋯YMt]t and Ym(m = 1,2,…,M) are N‐by‐K matrices. Next, we take Y as a data set composed of NM vertices where each vertex has K attributes. We perform the k‐means clustering on them and obtain the assignment label for each object in different graphs. Instead of using the block Laplacain matrix B, we can use the block intra‐normalized Laplacian matrix B^ in the above optimization problem.

(10)minYtrace(YtB^Y)subject toYtY=I

The algorithm is summarized as follows:

Algorithm 1

  1. Construct the block Laplacian matrix B or the block intra‐normalized Laplacian matrix B^;
  2. Compute the K eigenvectors y1,y2,…,yK corresponding to the K smallest eigenvalues of B (or B^) and construct Y = [y1,y2,…,yK];
  3. Perform the k‐means clustering on Y and generate K clusters on NM vertices. On each individual graph size, N vertices will be put into corresponding clusters in each Gm,1 ⩽ m ⩽ M.

We should mention Algorithm 1 is a standard spectral clustering procedure applied to the block matrix B or B^. Here, β plays a role in controlling the consistency of clustering in different graphs (refers to the discussion in Section 4.1). When β is large, the clustering tends to be consistent across different graphs. When β is small, the clustering tends to be more dependent on each graph cluster structure. It is obvious that when β = 0, the problem reduces to standard spectral clustering to each graph. We should also mention that the number of clusters in different graphs can be different in some applications. In such a case, K is chosen to be the largest number of clusters across all graphs.

We remark that when further assume that YmtYm=1MI for 1 ⩽ m ⩽ M, we still have YtY = I and Equation 9 can be rewritten as follows:

minY∑m=1MtraceYmtLmYm−β∑m,m′=1,m≠m′MtraceYmtYm′.

Such assumption is further discussed in Section 4.1, which is related to relaxation of multiple graphs cut problems. Because of the orthogonal constraints, we know

traceYmtYm′=∑k=1KYm(:,k)tYm′(:,k)∥Ym(:,k)∥2∥Ym′(:,k)∥2

We see that we are finding latent optimal subspaces Ym for Gm, while at the same time, these subspaces Ym should be correlated maximally. In other words, we search for clustering of vertices within each graph, but at the same time, they should be highly consistent across different graphs. The above formulation can also be applied to the normalized block Laplacian matrix.

4.1Multiple graphs cut

In this subsection, we would like to demonstrate that the optimization problem in Equation 9 is a relaxation of a multiple graphs cut problem.

Let {Cm(1),Cm(2),⋯,Cm(K)} be a partition of Gm (1 ⩽ m ⩽ M). We define the intra‐graph weight between Cm(k) and its complement Cm(k)‾ in Gm by

ΦmCm(k),Cm(k)‾=12∑i∈Cm(k),j∈Cm(k)‾(Am(i,j)+Am(j,i))

and the consistency weight between Cm(k) in Gm and Cm′(k) in Gm′ by

ΨCm(k),Cm′(k)=∑s∈Cm(k),t∈Cm′(k)s△t

where s△t is equal to 1 (or 0) if s = t(or s ≠ t). Its modified version involving the weighted degrees of the vertices s and t is given by

Ψ′Cm(k),Cm′(k)=∑s∈Cm(k),t∈Cm′(k)s△tdsmdtm′.

4.1.1Multiple graphs ratio cut

The ratio cut approach consists in choosing partitions that minimizes

(11)J1Cm(1),Cm(2),⋯,Cm(K)m=1M=1M∑k=1K∑m=1M1|Cm(k)|ΦmCm(k),Cm(k)‾−βM∑m,m′=1m≠m′M∑k=1K1|Cm(k)||Cm′(k)|ΨCm(k),Cm′(k)

where |C| denotes the number of elements in C. The first term in the right‐hand side of Equation 11 is to measure the intra‐graph weight cost when we partition Gk into two parts: Cm(k) and Cm(k)‾. The second term in the right‐hand side of Equation 11 is to measure how much difference is between Cm(k) in Gm and Cm′(k) in Gm′. The quantities 1|Cm(k)| and 1|Cm(k)||Cm′(k)| in both terms are accounted for the size of Cm(k) and Cm′(k).

Let us define the following K cluster‐indicator NM‐vectors y(k) (k = 1,2,…,K) as follows:

y(k)=y1(k),y2(k),⋯,ym(k)tym(k)=ym(k)(1),ym(k)(2),⋯,ym(k)(N)t

with

(12)ym(k)(i)=1M|Cm(k)|,i∈Cm(k),0,i∉Cm(k).

The NM‐by‐K matrix Y = [y(1)y(2)⋯y(K)] satisfies YtY=IK. Also {ym(1),ym(2),⋯,ym(K)} are orthogonal for 1 ⩽ m ⩽ M. We note that

This implies optimizing Equation 11 is equivalent to finding a minimum of Equation 9 with a special form of Equation 12. Thus, Equation 9 is a relaxation of Equation 11 without imposing a special form.

4.2The bounds for the optimal values of J1and J2

In this subsection, we provide the lower and upper bounds for the optimal values J1 and J2 in Equations 11 and 13, respectively, when K = 2.

Let us first analyze the bounds for J2. By considering 13, it is easy to verify that the following inequality:

(18)minJ2w(·)−maxJ2a(·)⩽minJ2(·)⩽minJ2w(·).

By the spectral graph theory,21 we have the classical result.

Lemma 1

Suppose the second smallest eigenvalue of normalized Laplacian matrix of an undirected graph G is equal to λ. Then we have

λ2⩽minCΦ(C,C¯)min{vol(C),vol(C¯)}⩽2λ

With this lemma, we have the following result.

Lemma 2

For K = 2, we have

1M∑m=1Mλ(m)2⩽minJ2w(·)⩽2M∑m=1M2λ(m)

where λ(m) is the second smallest eigenvalue of normalized Laplacian matrix of Gm.

Proof.

When K = 2, we know that

minJ2w{Cm(1),Cm(1)‾}m=1M=1M∑m=1MminΦmCm(1),Cm(1)‾volCm(1)+ΦmCm(1),Cm(1)‾volCm(1)‾.

It implies that

1M∑m=1MminΦmCm(1),Cm(1)‾minvolCm(1),volCm(1)‾⩽minJ2wCm(1),Cm(1)‾m=1M⩽2M∑m=1MminΦmCm(1),Cm(1)‾minvolCm(1),volCm(1)‾.

The theorem follows by applying the result in Lemma 2 to the above inequality.

Next we derive the upper bound of J2a(·).

Lemma 3

For K = 2, we have

maxJ2a(·)⩽2β(M−1)
Proof.

Without loss of generality, we set

volCm(1)=di1(m)+di2(m)+⋯+dip(m),volCm(2)=dip+1(m)+dip+2(m)+⋯+diN(m),volCm′(1)=dj1(m′)+dj2(m′)+⋯+djq(m′),volCm′(2)=djq+1(m′)+djq+2(m′)+⋯+djN(m′).

By the definition of Ψ′, we know that

Ψ′Cm(j),Cm′(j)=∑i∈Cm(j)∩Cm′(j)di(m)di(m′).

Therefore, J2a can be bounded by

βM∑m,m′=1m≠m′M∑i∈Cm(1)∩Cm′(1)di(m)di(m′)di1(m)+di2(m)+⋯+dip(m)dj1(m′)+dj2(m′)+⋯+djq(m′)+∑i∈Cm(2)∩Cm′(2)di(m)di(m′)dip+1(m′)+dip+2(m′)+⋯+diN(m′)djq+1(m′)+djq+2(m′)+⋯+djN(m′).

We can further show that the two terms in the above summation are less than or equal to 1. It implies that the upper bound of J2a is bounded by 2β(M − 1). The result follows.

By using Lemmas 2 and 3, we have the following theorem.

Theorem 4

For K = 2, we have

1M∑m=1Mλ(m)2−2β(M−1)⩽minJ2(·)⩽2M∑m=1M2λ(m).

It is clear when M = 1, the result is similar as the classical single graph result.21

Next, we discuss the lower and upper bounds of J1. Let dmin(m)=min1⩽i⩽Ndi(m) and dmax(m)=max1⩽i⩽Ndi(m). They refer to the minimal and maximal weighted degrees of the vertices in Gm, respectively. We can establish the following inequality:

dmin(m)volCm(k)⩽1|Cm(k)|⩽dmax(m)volCm(k),∀1⩽m⩽M,1⩽k⩽2.

By using 19 and similar argument in Theorem 4, we obtain the bounds for J1.

Theorem 5

For K = 2, we have

1M∑m=1Mdmin(m)λ(m)2−2β(M−1)⩽minJ1(·)⩽2M∑m=1Mdmax(m)2λ(m).

5.1Synthetic data

In this subsection, we use synthetic data sets to demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithms. We generate six synthetic graphs (M = 3) with 150 vertices in each graph, which consists of two clusters (TestsK2‐1 – TestsK2‐9) or three clusters (TestsK3‐1 – TestsK3‐9) shown in Table 3. The graphs are generated randomly, which are controlled by three parameters. One is the density parameter to control the number of edges within each group in the graph. The other one is the noise parameter to control the number of edges between the vertices across two clusters in the graph. The last one is the clustering coefficient,22 which is the measure of the extent to which one vertex's neighbors are also neighbors of each other. This measure assesses the degree to which nodes tend to cluster together. Here, we use it to control cluster structure in all the graphs. For instance, when density is equal to 20%, it means that 20% of A(i,j) within each group are set to be 1 randomly. When noise is equal to 5%, it means 5% of A(i,j) across two clusters are set to be 1 randomly. In Table 3, we show the sizes of the two clusters in each graph. For instance, (60,90), (50,100), and (40,110) mean that the sizes of the first clusters in G1, G2, and G3 are 60, 50, and 40, respectively, and the sizes of the second clusters in G1, G2 and G3 are 90, 100 and 110 respectively. RI=TP+TNTP+TN+FP+FN

where “TP” refers to true positive case that assigns vertices of the same group into the same cluster; “TN” refers to true negative case that assigns vertices of different clusters into different clusters; “FP” refers to false positive case that assigns vertices of different clusters into the same cluster; “FN” refers to false negative case that assigns vertices of the same group into different clusters.

6CONCLUDING REMARKS AND FUTURE WORK

In this paper, we study the problem of clustering with multiple graphs. We propose and formulate the block Laplacian matrix and determine a latent optimal subspace by considering both the clustering within each graph and the inherent consistency among clusters in different graphs. We have shown that our proposed clustering methods can be considered as relaxations of multiple graphs cut optimization problems. Also, we have presented the lower and upper bounds of the optimal solutions of multiple graphs cut so that the clustering results by the proposed block spectral clustering method can be compared. In the numerical results, the performance of the proposed block spectral clustering methods is better than the other tested clustering methods, for a problem in which there are K clusters in each graph that are similar but have some variation. Moreover, some interesting clusters can be found in a real publication data set.

This research also opens up several avenues for future work. In many applications, multiple graphs are collected from various sources and connected via inter‐relation, handling such graphs with different size is an important and challenging topic. Also in our framework, all graphs are equally used for data clustering. Thus, developing efficient algorithms automatically with graph selection should be an important topic of future research.
